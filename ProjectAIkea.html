Elsewhere Gallery

Daria Belkina

Human Creator Ledger

Pre-AI Concept: The initial idea was to explore the exhibition space as a liminal state of transition. I envisioned a video where the gallery becomes a portal and the paintings open into other dimensions. The camera was meant to move through the corridor, approach a central painting, and pass through it, creating an effect of immersion and transformation.

Deliberate Artistic Decisions: During the generation process, I constantly intervened to preserve the intended composition and atmosphere. The central painting was often replaced by random imagery generated by the AI. I manually edited the first and last frames in Photoshop, inserting the correct images. I also deliberately removed excessive details added by the model to maintain minimalism and a liminal atmosphere.

Post-AI Transformative Work: The final outcome is a continuous loop video composed of 12 transitions, where the camera moves through paintings, connecting spaces in an almost seamless way. The work became possible only through repeated manual corrections and multiple rounds of regeneration.

AI Co-Creator Profile

AI Models: Generative video models Kling and Sora were used. Photoshop was applied for editing static frames and preparing input images.

Creative Divergence: At crucial stages, the AI introduced unexpected “glitches” — such as random images inside the framed painting or distortions of architectural elements. These unpredictable results became points of divergence: some I corrected manually, while others I embraced as enriching visual discoveries.

Significance of AI Elements: The atmosphere of emptiness, the pink carpet floor, diffused light, and a sense of liminality emerged from AI generations, and I decided to keep them. They became an essential part of the work’s visual language.

Synthesis Process

Unexpected Contributions: The AI repeatedly substituted random imagery into the paintings, producing alien elements. These became surprising visual accents that I transformed and occasionally preserved, which added unpredictability to the final work.

Constraints & Human Intervention: The main limitation was that the AI could not create a seamless loop in a single generation. I had to develop a method of recursive generations:
1. Generate an initial still image of the space.
2. Generate a short video sequence of camera movement.
3. Manually edit the key first and last frames in Photoshop, replacing the painting with the correct imagery.
4. Re-run the video generation on the updated frames, effectively “tricking” the model.
5. Repeat this cycle across all 12 transitions.
This process became a true act of negotiation: the algorithm introduced randomness, while the human artist maintained conceptual and visual coherence.
